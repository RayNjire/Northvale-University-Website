LECTURERS

The lecturers at our school are judged by one mythology which ensure maximum lecturer-student coordination, and also entertaining and descriptive at the same time. It may be just a myth to others, but after getting a bit of experience in the teaching area, you too will find out that these myths may as well be true.

The one myth we use, apparently being one of the most persistent myths, is that some people are born with an extraordinary flair for lecturing. Our lecturers have shown more than just an interest in standing in front of the class room and talking, they accept challenges offered by students and the more the dramatic and problematic the questions by the students are, the more fascinating the class becomes.

You might be looking look for more of a performer if you want someone to be presenting at a conference or symposium, a person who is able to engage the audience and make the talk memorable. Well our teachers here are more than able to do that. They are sent all over the world to represent our school in various talk shows and international advertising of our school, that is, being sent to different schools around the world to tell others about the wonderful school we have.
Effective lecturing is more a matter of skill than charisma. The characteristics of our lecturers, as put down by the National Teacher’s Training Institute, are that they:

      1. Present the material in a clear and logical sequence
      2. Make the material accessible, intelligible and meaningful
      3. Cover the subject matter adequately
      4. Are constructive and helpful in their criticism
      5. Demonstrate an expert (and authoritarian) knowledge in their subject
      6. Pace the lecture appropriately
      7. Include material not readily accessible in textbooks
      8. Are concise illustrate the practical applications of the theory presented
      9. Show enthusiasm for the subject
      10. Generate curiosity about the lecture material early in the lecture.
      11. Present the audience with opportunities for meaningful engagement with the subject material and with their lecturer.

An effective lecture should present information that the audience could not learn from simply reading up on the subject of the lecture. Adding a presentation at a conference or workshop stimulates the audience to find out more or to introduce some new research or perspective. Bring your children over and they will confirm that the information given here is not a myth…
